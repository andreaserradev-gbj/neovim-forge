## The Corne's Week 1 Advantage

After 7 days of intensive Vim training, you've experienced something standard keyboard users don't: **hardware designed for Vim**.

Every skill you learned this week maps cleanly to the Corne:
- **hjkl navigation** is on the right home row — your fingers never move
- **Insert modes** are on the left home row — accessible without stretching
- **Operators** are on the left home row — immediate access
- **Escape (J+K)** is on the home row — no corner reach
- **Text objects** are letter keys — all on base layer
- **Search** is accessible — / on Raise, other searches on base layer

This alignment is why the Corne feels natural for Vim. The keyboard isn't fighting the editor—they work together.

---

## Corne Physical Muscle Memory After Week 1

By now, your hands should have established these patterns:

### Left Hand Patterns
- **Home row:** Insert modes (i, a), delete/change operators (d, c), and text object modifiers (i, a)
- **Upper row:** Motions (w, e), text objects (s, p), and backward motions (b)
- **Lower row:** Rarely used, or shifted for symbols

### Right Hand Patterns
- **Home row:** Navigation (hjkl), paste (p), copy operator (y)
- **Upper row:** More motions (e, w), find motions (f, t)
- **Lower row:** Find repeat (;, ,)

### Thumbs
- **Left thumb:** Modifier layers (Lower)
- **Right thumb:** Modifier layers (Raise), Escape combo (J+K), main layer (Space as leader for LazyVim next week)

Your hands know these positions. You don't think about them anymore.

## Speed and Ergonomics on the Corne vs Standard Keyboard

Here's what you should notice by Week 1 end:

**Speed:**
- Your editing is faster because you're not reaching for keys
- Navigation feels fluid because hjkl are home row
- Operators are instantly accessible

**Ergonomics:**
- Your hands rest on home row and barely move
- No corner reaches (Escape is J+K, not top-left)

**Confidence:**
- You trust the Corne. It doesn't fight you
- Layer switching (Lower, Raise, iCtrl/iAlt) is reflexive
- You're not thinking about the keyboard—you're thinking about the text

Standard keyboard users have to train these same skills while fighting keyboard geometry. You're training skills while being supported by hardware.

## Preparing for Week 2: Plugin Keys on Corne

Next week (Week 2), you'll learn LazyVim and plugins. This requires the **leader key**.

On the Corne, the leader key is typically mapped to Space (right thumb). This is ergonomic:

```
Leader key combinations on Corne:
Space (right thumb) + any letter on left side = two-key chord
Examples:
- Space + f (leader-f) for file search
- Space + g (leader-g) for git operations
- Space + s (leader-s) for search
```

All leader key combos are right thumb + left side letter. Your right hand is free to navigate while you hold leader on your right thumb.

This is **superior** to standard keyboards where Ctrl or Alt (corner keys) plus letters requires awkward hand contortion.

By the end of Week 1, confirm:
1. Your space bar (or leader key) is accessible with right thumb
2. You can hold it while tapping left-side letters with right hand free

## Corne Habits to Cement This Week

### Habit 1: Home Row is Home

Your hands should rest on home row 90% of the time. Between keystrokes, they're not floating or in weird positions. They're home.

### Habit 2: Layers Are Transparent

Lower, Raise, iCtrl/iAlt should feel like they don't exist. You need a number? Hold Lower, tap the number, done. You don't think about it.

### Habit 3: Escape Is J+K, Always

By now, reaching for physical Escape should feel wrong. Your J+K combo is automatic. You should be unable to *not* press them together when exiting insert mode.

### Habit 4: Motion Flows, Hand Position Doesn't

When navigating, your hands don't move. Only fingers tap keys. Navigation is a flow, not a physical journey across the board.

## Physical Checkup

After a week of intensive Vim training, check your physical state:

1. **Wrists:** Are they straight? Not bent inward or outward?
2. **Shoulders:** Are they relaxed? Not hunched?
3. **Forearms:** Are they resting on your desk or armrests comfortably?
4. **Fingers:** Are they in natural curl? Not stretched?
5. **Posture:** Are you sitting upright, not slouching?

The Corne's ergonomic design assumes good posture. If anything hurts, adjust your setup before continuing. Vim training should reduce strain, not increase it.

## Summary: Corne + Vim = Alignment

Week 1 was about learning Vim's grammar. The Corne was the canvas that made it comfortable.

You've discovered that:
- **Motion is natural** because hjkl are on home row
- **Operators are accessible** because d/c/y are home row
- **Insert mode is quick** because i/a/I/A/o/O are accessible
- **Escape is effortless** because J+K is home row
- **Text objects are instant** because they're letter keys
- **Search is smooth** because / and other keys have reasonable access

This alignment—hardware supporting editor philosophy—is rare in computing. That's why Vim on a split keyboard feels so natural.

You're not just learning Vim. You're experiencing Vim as it was meant to be: fast, ergonomic, and enabling flow.

Week 2 brings the power tools. You're ready.
